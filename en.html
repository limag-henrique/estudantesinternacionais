Documentation

You have already received (or will receive) a giant list of documents and a step-by-step guide to get them. Our country is famous for bureaucracy, but we will help you through this mess. The most important thing now is the CPF. Memorize this name well—you will hear it in markets, apps, pharmacies, at college, and even at the doctor's. CPF = Cadastro de Pessoas Físicas: it is an individual number used to identify a person in systems.

Unlike the US Social Security number, the CPF here is widely used to standardize registrations in companies, hospitals, and universities. You can apply for a CPF:

  • Before coming to Brazil: at the Brazilian consulate in your country (recommended to speed things up). See: https://www.gov.br/pt-br/servicos/inscrever-no-cpf-no-exterior. Tip: send the documents by email; they usually respond within 1–2 weeks.
  • After arriving: at Federal Revenue (Receita Federal) offices in Belo Horizonte.

Besides the CPF, you need the National Registry of Migration (CRNM). You have 30 days to register your migratory status with the Federal Police. To enroll at UFMG, you must present this document.

The CRNM works like an ID Card: you don't need to walk around with your passport all the time (just carry a printed copy for emergencies) — carry the CRNM.

Step-by-step to get the CRNM

On the scheduled day, bring:

  1. Filled form Link to form
  2. Two 3×4 photos, recent, color, with white background.
  3. Declaration of electronic address and contact means Link to declaration
  4. Copy of proof of residence (electricity bill, rent, internet, etc.) in your name, if you have one.
  5. Official identification document (passport, ID from country of origin with photo).
  6. Original Visa Application Form.
  7. Acceptance letter from UFMG.
  8. Proof of payment of the CRNM issuance fee (GRU). Revenue code: 140120. Reference value: R$ 204.77 (check at the time of issuance).

After having these documents, there is no need to walk around with your passport, just take a copy and always carry your CRNM.

Communication

Most people in BH speak only Portuguese. If you have trouble with the language, ask them to speak slower, repeat in English slowly, or use gestures; people usually help.

And don't worry! We aren't French; we don't feel ashamed or angry about not speaking Portuguese. When that happens, it's just because the person doesn't know the language, but in the vast majority of cases, people will be very helpful in assisting you in the best way possible, even if it's in broken English, Spanish, or French.

Banking

With CPF and a cell phone, it's time to open your bank account!

  • PIX: instant payment system used by everyone (no fees for individuals) — it's like a government-run Venmo/Zelle.
  • Banco Inter: one of the most used digital banks; allows receiving national and international SWIFT/ACH transfers without fees, easy exchange, investments, and debit card in BRL and USD.
  • Revolut, Wise, Nubank: other bank options with debit cards, easy exchange, and online opening.
  • Banco do Brasil: Open a UNIVERSITY account. Foreigners can apply for a credit card more easily here. It is the most suitable for paying fees, fines, and the UFMG club monthly fee.

All options above have accounts without basic costs, but be careful with credit, as it can generate debt quickly.

Physical money (cash)? Almost no one uses it, but it's worth having some bills for emergencies.

Here the tipping culture is not common: the 10% service fee usually comes included in the bill. If you didn't like the service, ask to remove the 10%.

When paying with a card, they will ask if you want "débito" (debit) or "crédito" (credit). If you have money in the account, choose debit; if you use the credit function at Banco do Brasil, pay the bill on time to avoid problems.

With PIX you make instant payments via QR Code or link — great for splitting bills with friends.

Transport

  • Google Maps works very well for bus routes in BH; just simulate the route and click on the public transport icon.
  • Municipal buses (Blue): cost R$ 6.25. You can pay in cash or with a BHBus card (rechargeable card at stations and in the BHBus+ app).
  • BRT (Green/Yellow buses): use the MUNICIPAL platforms in the middle of the avenue, are faster, and have the same price as municipal ones. Attention! There are direct buses and "paradores" (marked with P); the latter stop at all stations.
  • Intermunicipal buses (Red, Orange, Purple): accept only cash or "Ótimo" card, useful if you are going to other cities nearby.
  • Metro: our "metro" works like a surface train. Payment with card (credit/debit), BHBus, Ótimo, or cash. There is one line (directions Novo Eldorado — Contagem; Vilarinho — Venda Nova).
  • Shared electric bike stations: for about R$ 35.00/month you are entitled to 4 trips of 30 minutes each per day (TemBici app) — payment via credit card. Interesting if you walk a lot around the Center or Pampulha Lagoon.
  • Transport apps: use Uber and 99. Activate PIN authentication on the ride for safety. Prefer paying by card or PIX, avoid cash.
  • Delivery: most used apps are Ifood and 99Food.

Safety

You chose well: BH is relatively calm and is a university, gastronomic, and cultural hub. Still, being a large region, urban problems happen.

  • Most common crime: pickpockets. Violent robberies are less frequent, but they exist.
  • Tips: walk accompanied, during the day in busy places; keep your cell phone and documents in fanny packs, inside pockets, or tightly closed backpacks.
  • When using your cell phone on the street, step into a store if you need to make a payment, call someone, or check the GPS.

Safest neighborhoods: South Zone (Savassi, Lourdes, Mangabeiras), Buritis, Pampulha, UFMG, and Belvedere. Neighborhoods to redouble attention: Centro (Downtown), Cachoeirinha, and Venda Nova.

Parks, museums, malls, and stores in general are usually safe — the important thing is not to give opportunity to thieves.

Health

Got the flu? Dengue? Yellow fever? Need condoms? Good news: Brazil has free public health for anyone in the country.

The SUS (Unified Health System) works on two levels:

UBS (Basic Health Unit): for general consultations, flu, vaccines, condoms, etc. Bring CRNM, CPF, and proof of residence. Each address has a reference UBS — send me your street name and I'll check the nearest one and opening hours for you.

UPA (Emergency Care Unit): when things are really serious. In Minas, we usually say "o trem tá feio" (the train is ugly) — a local slang; get used to it, because here, everything can be a "trem" (train/thing). The UBS can refer you to the UPA, or you can go on your own if you think it's necessary.

Besides SUS, you likely have private health insurance with national coverage. Check the grace period and coverage (where you can be attended), and know the units closest to you.

Tip: take advantage of the fact that vaccines are offered at UBS and take the ones you haven't had yet.
